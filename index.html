Visa & Residence Permits

Before you can live and work in Switzerland, you need the right permit. Requirements differ significantly between EU/EFTA and non-EU nationals.

⚠️
Non-EU/EFTA nationals: You cannot simply move to Switzerland — you need a job offer first. Your employer must prove no suitable local candidate exists before applying for your work permit.
EU/EFTA Citizens
Confirm EU/EFTA citizenship — free movement applies
EU/EFTA nationals can enter Switzerland freely and stay up to 3 months while job searching, with no visa required. After finding work, apply for a B or L permit directly at the commune.
EU/EFTA only
Register with commune and apply for permit after accepting a job
Once employed, EU/EFTA nationals register at the Einwohnerkontrolle and receive an L (short stay) or B (1+ year) permit in about 4 weeks.

Non-EU/EFTA Citizens
Secure a job offer from a Swiss employer first
Non-EU nationals must have a signed employment contract before applying. Your employer applies for a work permit at the cantonal migration office. Switzerland issues ~8,500 new B-permits annually for non-EU workers.
Required first
Employer applies for work permit at cantonal authority (Migrationsamt)
Your employer submits the application with proof that no suitable EU/EFTA/Swiss candidate was available. Approval goes from the canton to the State Secretariat for Migration (SEM).
Non-EU only
Collect entry visa from Swiss embassy in your home country
Once approved, collect your visa in person at the Swiss representation. You cannot enter Switzerland to work until this is done. Some nationalities (Japan, NZ, Singapore) only need a residence permit, not a visa.

After registering at your commune, your permit card is mailed in ~4 weeks. Keep it — you'll need it to open a bank account, sign contracts, and more.

Moving & Deregistering

Before leaving your home country, officially deregister and handle Swiss customs formalities for bringing your belongings across the border.

In your home country
Officially deregister your address (Abmeldung)
Visit your local civil registry or municipality. This stops local taxes and social security. You need a deregistration certificate for Swiss tax authorities.
Legal requirement
Notify pension and social security systems
Inform your home country's pension authority. Bilateral agreements may allow you to transfer or preserve contributions for future withdrawal.
Don't forget
Cancel or transfer local contracts (phone, insurance, subscriptions)
Give notice as per contractual deadlines — many require 1–3 months' notice. Set up mail forwarding with your national postal service.
Plan ahead
Customs & Shipping
Prepare inventory for customs (Form 18.44)
All household goods must be declared. Submit Form 18.44 to Swiss customs. Items owned for 6+ months are duty-free. Your moving company can assist with this process.
Customs required
Check rules for newer items, cars, and pets
Items owned less than 6 months may incur customs fees. Cars under 6 months old: significant import duty. Pets need microchipping, rabies vaccination, and health certificates. Check FSVO website.
Check carefully

How to Register in Switzerland (Anmeldung)

Registering at your local municipality (Gemeinde / Commune / Comune) within 14 days of arrival is mandatory — everything else depends on it. You need your passport, rental contract, employment contract, and passport photos. EU/EFTA citizens get a confirmation on the spot; non-EU citizens receive a permit card within 2–4 weeks.

Within 14 days of arrival
Register at the Einwohnerkontrolle / Contrôle des habitants
Bring: passport/ID, rental contract or accommodation proof, employment contract, and passport photos. You receive a confirmation; permit card follows in ~4 weeks.
14-day deadline
Register all family members (spouse, children)
Each family member must register. Bring marriage certificate and birth certificates with certified translations. Only then can you claim Kinderzulagen (child allowance ~ CHF 200–250/month per child).
Families
Within 30 days
Notify employer of your permit number for payroll
Your employer needs your permit number to set up AHV/AVS social security deductions and withholding tax. Upload a permit copy to your HR system.
Work requirement
Register for Serafe (TV/radio licence fee)
Every household in Switzerland pays CHF 335/year to Serafe — regardless of whether you own a TV. You'll typically receive a letter automatically after registering at the commune.
Mandatory
Verify AHV number (Swiss social insurance number)
Your AHV/AVS number is Switzerland's social security number. Check it on your first payslip and ensure it's correct — errors are very difficult to fix retroactively.

TWINT — essential Swiss payment

TWINT is the Swiss mobile payment standard — used at markets, small shops, restaurants, parking machines, and vending machines. Every Swiss bank has its own TWINT app. Set it up on day one. Yuh has its own dedicated TWINT app (easier to use); Neon relies on the TWINT prepaid app (must top up in advance).

🧾

QR-bills & eBill

Switzerland replaced the old orange/red payment slips with QR-bills in 2022. Scan the QR code in your banking app to pay any bill instantly. Set up eBill (electronic bill delivery) through your bank to receive invoices digitally and pay with one click — most major billers support it.

💼

Quellensteuer (withholding tax)

If you have a B permit, your employer deducts income tax directly from your salary each month (Quellensteuer). You don't file a standard tax return — but you can request a correction if you have deductions (like 3a, mortgage interest, or childcare). Once you earn over CHF 120,000/year or have a C permit, you file a normal tax return.

🏦

Three-pillar pension system

Pillar 1 (AHV): state pension, mandatory. Pillar 2 (BVG): occupational pension via employer, mandatory for employees. Pillar 3a: voluntary private savings, tax-deductible up to CHF 7,258/year (2026). A new 2026 rule allows buying back missed contributions from 2025 onward — start contributing now to build a buffer you can retroactively fill.

Vehicle & Driving Licence

If you have a foreign driving licence or are bringing a car, there are strict deadlines. Missing them leads to fines and complications.

Driving licence exchange
Exchange foreign licence within 12 months
All foreign licence holders must exchange to a Swiss licence within 12 months of establishing residency. EU/EFTA licences: no driving test required. Non-EU: may need theory exam or driving test depending on your country.
12-month deadline
Gather documents for the Strassenverkehrsamt
Required: application form, biometric passport photo, eye test certificate (from any optician), residence permit copy, and original foreign licence. Process through your cantonal road traffic office.

Vehicle registration & driving rules

Foreign-plated cars can be used for up to 1 year. After that: register at cantonal Strassenverkehrsamt. Bring vehicle docs, proof of insurance, customs declaration, and Swiss ID.

Parking in Switzerland

Blue zones: free with parking disc (1–2h limit). White zones: paid parking. Yellow zones: restricted. Annual residential permits available from municipalities.

⚡

Speed limits & fines

Built-up areas: 50 km/h. Outside built-up: 80 km/h. Motorways: 120 km/h. Swiss fines are income-proportional — high earners face very large speeding penalties.

Moving to Switzerland

The most common questions from expats arriving in Switzerland — answered.

How do I register in Switzerland (Anmeldung)?

You must register at your local Gemeinde (municipality) within 14 days of moving in. Bring your passport or ID, rental contract, employment contract, and passport photos. EU/EFTA citizens receive a confirmation on the spot; non-EU citizens receive a permit card within 2–4 weeks. Missing the deadline can result in a fine.

How do I choose health insurance in Switzerland?

You have 90 days from arrival to choose a health insurer — coverage is backdated to day one. Basic KVG/LAMal coverage is legally identical at all insurers, so only the price differs. Compare premiums at priminfo.ch. Consider a CHF 2,500 franchise with a Telmed model to save up to 30% on premiums if you're healthy. Switch before November 30 each year for January 1 changes.

What is the best bank account for expats in Switzerland?

Yuh (powered by Swissquote) and Neon are the top free choices — both offer a genuine Swiss IBAN, TWINT support, and low FX fees. UBS key4 Pure is best if you want a traditional bank with English support. Avoid Revolut and N26 — they do not provide a personal Swiss IBAN, which most employers and landlords require for salary and deposit payments.

Do I need a work permit to move to Switzerland?

EU/EFTA citizens can live and work in Switzerland freely and register directly at the Gemeinde. Non-EU citizens require a work permit (B or L permit) sponsored by a Swiss employer before arrival. Permits are quota-controlled for certain nationalities and typically take 6–14 weeks to process depending on the canton.

How much does it cost to live in Switzerland?

Expect CHF 2,400–5,000+ per month for a single person in Zürich: rent CHF 1,600–3,000+, health insurance CHF 310–600+, groceries CHF 300–750, and transport CHF 80–360. Swiss salaries are proportionally very high — the median gross salary is around CHF 6,788/month. Living in Bern, Winterthur, or Lucerne is 20–30% cheaper than Zürich for rent.

What is Pillar 3a and should expats use it?

Pillar 3a is Switzerland's voluntary tax-deferred retirement account. You can contribute up to CHF 7,258 per year (2026 limit), fully deducted from taxable income — saving around CHF 1,300–1,500 in taxes annually. Invest via VIAC, finpension, or Frankly for ETF-based returns of ~5–7% historically. When you leave Switzerland you can withdraw the full amount at a reduced tax rate (5–8%), making it worthwhile even for temporary expats.

Frequently asked questions
Do I really have to get Swiss health insurance? ▾

Yes. Basic health insurance (Grundversicherung) is mandatory for all residents of Switzerland within 90 days of registering. If you miss the deadline, your canton can assign you an insurer automatically, and you may face a premium surcharge.

My employer gives me accident insurance — do I still need health insurance? ▾

Yes, health insurance is still mandatory. However, if you're employed more than 8 hours per week with one employer, your employer covers workplace accidents (UVG). In this case, you should remove accident coverage from your health insurance plan — it saves you roughly 7% on premiums.

When can I switch health insurance provider? ▾

Once a year: give notice by 30 November and coverage switches on 1 January. Exception: if your insurer raises your premium, you have 30 days from receiving the letter to switch. Insurers must accept you regardless of health status for basic insurance.

What is supplemental insurance and do I need it? ▾

Supplemental insurance (Zusatzversicherung) covers extras not in basic insurance: private hospital room, dental, vision, worldwide coverage, alternative medicine, gym reimbursements, etc. It's voluntary. Insurers CAN reject you or add exclusions based on your health. Apply early, before any conditions arise. SWICA and CSS have popular expat supplemental packages.

Can I get financial help paying premiums? ▾

Yes — called Prämienverbilligung (IPV). Households with lower incomes can apply to their canton for a subsidy. Every canton has different thresholds. Apply at your cantonal office soon after arriving. Around 30% of Swiss residents receive some subsidy.

Is my health insurance valid in other countries? ▾

Emergency care is covered in EU/EFTA countries (up to twice Swiss rates). Outside EU, basic insurance doesn't cover you. If you travel frequently, add international supplemental coverage or a travel insurance policy.

Which bank should I open first as a new arrival? ▾

Open Neon or Yuh first — both open in minutes via app, give you a proper Swiss IBAN, and are free. Use this for your salary. Then open a second account (e.g. Migros Bank) as backup. Add Wise for international transfers and avoid Revolut/N26 as your primary Swiss account.

When should I start Pillar 3a? ▾

As soon as you have your first payslip and know you'll stay in Switzerland. Even CHF 200/month invested in a VIAC or finpension ETF portfolio compounds enormously over 5–30 years, plus you get the full tax deduction every year.

Is there capital gains tax on stocks in Switzerland? ▾

No — private capital gains are not taxed in Switzerland. However, dividends are subject to 35% withholding tax (Verrechnungssteuer). You claim most of this back in your tax return. As a B permit holder, some may be withheld. Consult a tax advisor for large portfolios.

What is the 3-pillar pension system? ▾

Pillar 1 (AHV): state pension — mandatory contributions from salary. Pillar 2 (BVG/Pensionskasse): occupational pension — employer + employee contribute. Pillar 3a: private voluntary savings — tax-deductible contributions, invest freely. As a new arrival, Pillars 1 & 2 are automatic from your employment. Start Pillar 3a yourself.

What does household contents insurance NOT cover? ▾

Structural damage to the building itself (covered by building insurance, usually included in rent), vehicles, high-value jewelry above policy limits, and items you leave unsupervised in public. Always read the exclusions before buying.

My employer has accident insurance — what do I still need? ▾

If you work 8+ hours per week for one employer, they cover workplace accidents (UVG). Third-party liability (Haftpflicht) is legally mandatory before you can register a car. Partial casco (Teilkasko) adds theft and weather damage. Full casco (Vollkasko) also covers your own collision damage. Premiums depend on car value, your canton, claim history, and annual mileage. Compare on Comparis or moneyland.ch.

Should I buy a Halbtax or a GA? ▾

Frequently asked questions
Why doesn't Migros sell alcohol? ▾

This is a founding principle of Migros, established by founder Gottlieb Duttweiler in 1925. As a co-operative, Migros has maintained this policy out of ethical principles. Their subsidiary Denner, however, does sell alcohol — and Denner is widespread across Switzerland.

Is cross-border grocery shopping legal? ▾

Yes — perfectly legal. Swiss residents can import up to CHF 300 worth of goods duty-free per person per day from EU countries. Meat has additional weight limits (1 kg per person). You must declare goods exceeding the threshold at the border. Keep receipts.

Can I shop on Sunday anywhere? ▾

Main supermarkets are closed Sunday. Open options include: train station shops (SBB Migros and Coop branches open Sunday), motorway service stations, petrol station shops, some convenience stores (k kiosk), and specialist stores at airports. Sunday shopping is a cultural adjustment most expats have to make.

Are Swiss supermarkets cash only? ▾

No — Migros, Coop, Aldi, and Lidl all accept debit cards, credit cards, and TWINT. Cash is accepted everywhere too. Only small stalls, market vendors, some bakeries and butchers, and older local shops may be cash or TWINT only.

    How to Register in Switzerland (Anmeldung)
    Registration is legally required within 14 days of moving into a new home.
    📋

    What you need

    Passport/ID, rental contract or landlord confirmation (Wohnungsgeberbestätigung), passport photo, and your work contract or permit. Non-EU citizens also need their visa/residence permit.

    ⏱️

    Timeline

    Book your Gemeinde appointment online (wait times vary: 1–10 days). EU/EFTA residents get a permit on the spot. Non-EU residents may need to wait 2–4 weeks for the permit card to arrive.

    💡

    Key tip

    Register in the canton where you actually live, not where you work. Your canton determines your tax rate, health insurance premiums, and school district. Some cantons (e.g. Zug, Schwyz) are significantly cheaper.

    ⚠️

    Don't miss

    Missing the 14-day deadline can result in a fine. Your registration also triggers your health insurance obligation — you must choose an insurer within 3 months of arriving or the canton assigns one for you.

    Choosing Health Insurance in Switzerland
    The single most expensive decision you make each year — and the one most expats get wrong.
    💰

    The premium trap

    Basic insurance (KVG/LAMal) covers identical treatments at every insurer — the only legal difference is price and model. Paying CHF 580/month with Helsana when Assura offers CHF 310 for the same coverage is a CHF 3,240/year mistake.

    📅

    The November deadline

    You can switch insurer or franchise every year — but only before November 30 for January 1. Miss this deadline and you're locked in for another year. Set a calendar reminder now.

    🔑

    Franchise strategy

    If you're healthy and rarely visit the doctor, choose CHF 2,500 franchise + Telmed model. This cuts your monthly premium by up to 30%. The "break even" point: if your annual doctor costs stay below ~CHF 1,800, higher franchise wins.

    🌍

    Supplemental insurance

    Basic KVG only covers emergencies abroad (EU only, up to 2× Swiss rates). If you travel frequently or want private hospital rooms, add a supplemental (Zusatzversicherung) plan — but read the fine print on exclusion periods for pre-existing conditions.

    Best Bank Accounts for Expats
    Swiss banks range from free digital accounts to CHF 300/year legacy fees — know the difference before you sign up.
    📱

    Neon (our top pick for most)

    Free account, CHF 0 foreign transaction fee within the EU, Maestro + Mastercard included, instant notifications, English app. Perfect for day-to-day spending. Use code SWISSNEWBIE for up to CHF 35 bonus.

    🏢

    Traditional banks (UBS, PostFinance)

    Worth it if your employer pays fees, you need a mortgage, or you want a branch relationship. PostFinance is required for many direct debit setups. UBS is dominant for wealth management and expats with complex needs.

    💸

    International transfers

    Never use a Swiss bank for international transfers — fees of CHF 5–20 per transfer plus unfavourable exchange rates add up fast. Use Wise (TransferWise) instead: mid-market rate, low flat fee, often 10× cheaper.

    ⚠️

    Revolut warning

    Revolut is not a Swiss bank and operates on an EU e-money licence. It's fine for travel but shouldn't be your primary account in Switzerland — salary payments, direct debits, and some Swiss services require a proper CH IBAN.

    Pillar 3a — The Tax-Saving Retirement Account Every Expat Needs
    Contributing to Pillar 3a is the easiest legal tax saving available in Switzerland — yet many expats ignore it for years.
    💡

    How it works

    You contribute up to CHF 7,258/year (2026 limit). This amount is fully deducted from your taxable income. At a 20% marginal rate, that's CHF 1,450 in instant tax savings. The money is invested and grows tax-free until retirement or when you leave Switzerland.

    🏦

    Bank vs. securities account

    A bank 3a (PostFinance, UBS) earns near-zero interest. A securities 3a (VIAC, finpension, Frankly) invests in equities with ~5–7% historical returns. Over 20 years, the difference is enormous — choose securities unless retirement is very close.

    ✈️

    Leaving Switzerland

    When you leave, you can withdraw your Pillar 3a in full — even if you're years from retirement. Tax is charged at a reduced "lump-sum withdrawal" rate, typically 5–8% depending on canton. This makes 3a worthwhile even for expats planning to leave.

    📅

    Deadline: December 31

    Contributions must be made before December 31 each year. You can't backfill previous years (unlike some other countries). Even a small contribution in your first year saves tax — start as early as possible.

    Moving to Switzerland — The Complete Checklist
    Everything before and after you arrive — in the right order.
    ✈️

    Before you arrive

    Non-EU/EFTA: secure your work permit before flying. Organise your rental contract (essential for Anmeldung). Ship only what you need — Swiss customs is strict on used goods over CHF 300 in value without proof of prior ownership.

    📌

    First 2 weeks

    Register at your Gemeinde (Anmeldung) within 14 days. Open a Swiss bank account — your employer will need a CH IBAN for your first salary. Get a Swiss SIM card (Sunrise, Salt, or Swisscom).

    🗓️

    First 3 months

    Choose your health insurer within 90 days of arrival (or the canton assigns one — likely the most expensive option). Start your Pillar 3a. Get your Halbtax card if you'll use public transport regularly.

    🚗

    First 12 months

    Exchange your foreign driving licence within 12 months (EU licences can be exchanged directly; non-EU may require a theory test). Deregister (Abmeldung) from your home country if required to avoid double taxation.

    Frequently asked questions
    The questions every new expat in Switzerland asks — answered honestly.
    Do I need to speak German to live in Switzerland? ▾
    Not to get started — English is widely spoken in Zürich, Geneva, Basel and most international workplaces. However, German (or French in Romandy, Italian in Ticino) makes daily life dramatically easier: apartment viewings, Gemeinde appointments, neighbours, and doctors all go more smoothly in the local language. Even basic conversational German is appreciated. Apps like Duolingo or Babbel are a good start; many employers also offer language subsidies.
    How long does it take to get a residence permit? ▾
    EU/EFTA citizens: register at the Gemeinde and receive a temporary confirmation immediately. The formal permit card (B or L permit) arrives by post within 2–4 weeks. Non-EU citizens: your employer must apply for a work permit before you arrive — this takes 6–14 weeks depending on canton and quota availability. Keep your passport and a certified copy of your permit on you until the card arrives.
    What's the biggest financial mistake new expats make? ▾
    Not comparing health insurance — by far. Basic (KVG) insurance covers identical treatments at every insurer by law, yet premiums vary by up to 40% for the same coverage. Spending 30 minutes on Priminfo.ch before November 30 can save CHF 1,000–2,500 per year. The second biggest mistake: not starting Pillar 3a immediately. Even a partial contribution in year one is better than missing the December 31 deadline.
    Is Switzerland part of the EU? ▾
    No — Switzerland is not an EU member, but is part of the Schengen Area (passport-free travel) and has around 120 bilateral agreements with the EU. In practice this means: EU citizens can live and work freely in Switzerland, Switzerland uses the Swiss Franc (CHF) not the Euro, VAT is 8.1% (lower than most EU countries), customs limits are CHF 300 when shopping abroad, and some EU financial regulations don't apply — which benefits the banking sector.
    How does Swiss taxation work for expats? ▾
    Most newly-arrived non-Swiss citizens who are not C permit holders pay tax at source (Quellensteuer) — deducted directly from salary by the employer, similar to PAYE. If your income exceeds CHF 120,000/year or you have additional income, you must file a tax return. Switzerland has a wealth tax (Vermögenssteuer) on top of income tax — unique in Europe. Tax rates vary significantly by canton: Zug and Schwyz are among the lowest, Geneva and Vaud among the highest.
    Can I bring my pet to Switzerland? ▾
    Yes, but with paperwork. EU pets need a valid EU Animal Passport, microchip, and up-to-date rabies vaccination. Non-EU pets additionally need a blood test confirming vaccine efficacy (titre test), done at least 30 days before travel. Dogs must be registered with the cantonal database within 10 days of arrival. Some breeds (e.g. American Pit Bull Terrier) are banned in certain cantons — check cantonal regulations before moving.
    What's the Sunday rule in Switzerland? ▾
    Switzerland takes Sundays seriously. Most shops are closed (exceptions: airports, train stations, some tourist areas). Laundry, loud DIY, mowing the lawn, and car washing on Sundays can result in neighbour complaints or, in apartment buildings, formal warnings. Restaurants and parks are open. If you're hosting, keep noise to reasonable levels after 10pm on weekdays and 11pm on weekends.
    How hard is it to find an apartment in Switzerland? ▾
    Very competitive, especially in Zürich, Zug, and Geneva. Vacancy rates are often below 1%. Tips: register on Homegate.ch, Immoscout24.ch and ImmoStreet.ch immediately; have your documents ready (salary slips, Betreibungsregisterauszug — a debt register extract, ID, and reference letter from previous landlord); apply to multiple listings at once; consider a sublet or furnished flat for the first 3–6 months while you search. Your Betreibungsregisterauszug is free and can be requested from your local debt registry office.
